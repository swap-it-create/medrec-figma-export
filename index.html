01 — Overview

The Project at a Glance

↻  Full Rewrite · Not a Redesign

MedRec is a clinical medication review platform used by US healthcare organizations to manage home visit medication therapy management (MTM) programs — primarily under Medicare Part D and physician-referred cases.

When I joined the project, the existing system had been in production for years with no meaningful UX investment. The interface was dense, inconsistent, and architected around database schemas rather than clinical thinking. Reviewers were opening 3–4 browser tabs to complete a single case. Admins were maintaining shadow spreadsheets alongside the tool because the assignment queue told them nothing useful.

This wasn't a cosmetic refresh. The product required a complete rethink, encompassing information architecture, user flows, component design, accessibility standards, and tablet-first responsiveness. I owned the process end to end, from initial research through design QA.

01.0 — Project Context

Two Phases. One Complete Redesign.

This was not a single greenfield engagement. Understanding the two-phase structure is essential context for every scope and research decision that follows.

Phase 1 · Q1 2025
Technology Migration
Microsoft framework reaching end-of-support. Stakeholder mandate: migrate to a new stack, introduce the design system and brand language, replicate the existing product with bare-minimum UX changes. No redesign. Framework chosen: .NET MAUI — cross-platform, within the existing Microsoft ecosystem. Due to budget and developer capacity constraints, .NET MAUI allowed the migration to proceed within existing team skills without a full technology pivot or additional hiring.
Outcome: Design system built from scratch. Migration complete. Structural problems preserved — now more visible in a clean UI.
→
Phase 2 · Jan 2026

Stakeholders confirmed full rewrite in React Native — single codebase, web and tablet parity. I was given 3 weeks for user research, synthesis, and design direction before development began. Every research method was chosen to produce decision-enabling insight within the sprint.

01.1 — Design Challenge

How Might I?

Every design project needs a North Star — a single, honest question that defines the problem space before a single pixel is placed. Without it, a case study reads as a collection of decisions. With it, every decision becomes an answer.

The North Star Question

"How might I design a clinical medication review tool that works reliably in offline home environments, handles complex polypharmacy cases with precision and speed, and requires zero onboarding for a time-pressured clinician — while remaining HIPAA-compliant, WCAG 2.2 AA accessible, and deployable without native app infrastructure?"

Sub-Challenge 01
The Connectivity Problem

Home visit environments have unpredictable cellular and Wi-Fi access. Any data loss during an active medication review is not a UX inconvenience; it is a patient safety event. The tool must behave identically whether the reviewer has full LTE or no connection at all, and sync without conflict when connectivity returns.

Sub-Challenge 02
The Clinical Complexity Problem

Home medication review patients average 8–12 active medications managed across multiple prescribers, pharmacies, and care settings. The tool must surface this clinical complexity (drug classes, dosage schedules, refill windows, contraindication flags) in a format a reviewer can process under time pressure in someone's living room.

Sub-Challenge 03
The Adoption Problem

Target users are licensed clinicians, not technology adopters. They are replacing a paper-and-binder workflow they have used for years. The new tool competes with the reliability of paper. It wins only if it is faster, more accurate, and requires no learning curve. The interface must explain itself through the quality of its design.

Sub-Challenge 04
The Compliance Problem

HIPAA, CMS billing requirements, WCAG 2.2 AA, Section 508, and state-level pharmacy regulations create a non-negotiable constraint envelope. These are not checkboxes applied at the end of the design process. They are forces that reshape every decision — from the color token system to the session timeout flow.

01.2 — Existing System Audit

What Was Already Broken

Before opening Figma, I ran a heuristic analysis of the existing system — every screen, both user roles (reviewer and admin) — mapped against Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics and WCAG 2.2 AA criteria. Eight failure patterns emerged. These findings directly shaped the scope decisions that follow.

Fragmented Case Workflow
Reviewers opened 3–4 tabs to complete a single case review. No unified case view existed — clinical data, patient history, and submission lived in separate screens with no navigation thread between them.
Schema-Driven Navigation
The information architecture mirrored the database — not the clinical workflow. Users had to learn how the system was structured before they could do their job. New reviewers needed onboarding that should have been unnecessary.
Broken Tablet Experience
Zero responsive design. Home visit reviewers — conducting medication reviews on-site at patient homes — were using a desktop-only UI on touch screens. Interaction targets reached as small as 14px. No offline capability existed.
Zero Accessibility
Failed WCAG 2.2 AA across every critical flow. Form inputs had no associated labels. Focus states were invisible. Contrast ratios fell below 3:1 throughout — in a clinical product used by healthcare professionals with varying visual needs.
No Admin Tooling
No admin interface existed. Operations staff managed reviewer assignments, caseloads, and deadlines entirely through external spreadsheets and manual coordination — with no visibility into case status, reviewer capacity, or submission progress.
⏱
No Urgency Signals
Time-sensitive cases — insurance deadlines, high-risk patients, compliance flags — appeared identically to routine cases in every queue. Priority was invisible. Reviewers had no way to triage without external context.
No System Status Feedback
Submitting a review gave no visible confirmation. Saves were silent. Reviewers routinely re-submitted completed cases fearing data loss — a direct violation of Nielsen's visibility of system status heuristic, and a patient safety risk in a clinical context.
Destructive Record Lock on Check-In
Opening a patient record required a manual check-in that placed an exclusive lock — blocking everyone, including admins, from accessing that record until check-out. A forgotten check-out meant the record stayed locked indefinitely, changes never synced, and no one could act on it. A single oversight held the entire patient record hostage.

05 — Secondary Research
The Industry Data That Made the Brief Undeniable.

Before proposing a single design decision, I grounded the project in regulatory requirements, peer-reviewed clinical literature, and published UX research. These weren't background reading — they became the language used in every stakeholder conversation.

CMS / MTM Regulatory Requirements
CMS mandates Medication Therapy Management for Medicare Part D patients on 2+ chronic conditions and 8+ medications. Every delayed or undocumented visit is a billable loss and a compliance event — not just a UX inconvenience.
HIPAA / HITECH Compliance
WhatsApp does not sign a BAA and is not HIPAA-compliant for PHI transmission. The existing workflow was an active federal violation. HITECH penalties reach $1.9M per violation category per year — this reframed WhatsApp replacement from a UX improvement to a legal obligation.
Clinical Complexity & Polypharmacy
41% of adults over 65 manage 5+ medications across multiple prescribers. Home medication reviewers routinely handle 10–14 active medications per patient. Medication non-adherence costs the US healthcare system $528B annually — confirming this is a patient safety domain, not a documentation one.
Healthcare UX Research (NNG / HIMSS / AMIA)
NNG: clinicians abandon tools requiring 3+ steps to core functionality. HIMSS 2023: 74% of healthcare orgs cite workflow inefficiency as primary barrier to digital adoption. AMIA: progressive disclosure reduces documentation errors by 32% vs all-fields-visible forms. Each finding directly shaped a design decision.
Offline-First Design Precedents
PouchDB/CouchDB sync patterns used in MSF field clinical tools established the benchmark for conflict-free offline sync. Google Workbox architecture informed the PWA-compatible approach. These weren't theoretical — they were proven in equally hostile connectivity environments.
WCAG 2.2 AA + Section 508
Healthcare applications serving Medicare/Medicaid populations are legally required to meet accessibility standards. The existing system failed both WCAG 2.2 AA and Section 508 — in a clinical context where accessibility is both a legal obligation and a patient safety requirement.
CMS Conditions of Participation
CMS CoP requires all patient contacts and clinical notes be documented contemporaneously — at the time of visit, not re-entered hours later from paper. The existing paper-first workflow was a direct CMS violation. Real-time offline documentation was not a UX preference — it was a federal requirement.
06 — Key Insights
Six Moments That Changed the Direction.

Not everything in research is equally important. These six findings directly caused a design or scope decision.

Insight 01
The real competition was WhatsApp — not the old app.
Reviewers weren't asking for a better tool. They had already built a working parallel system in WhatsApp. The new app had to be more reliable than a messaging platform before it could be more clinical than one.
→ Offline-first became a hard requirement
Insight 02
Reviewers lost 1hr 15min before seeing a single patient.
The daily hospital trip for assignment wasn't mentioned as a problem — it had been normalised. Service data confirmed it. For a 10-case day, the overhead made full completion structurally impossible.
→ Digital assignment added to v1 scope
Insight 03
90% abandonment — and management didn't know.
No analytics existed. The system appeared used because accounts were active. In reality the clinical workflow had moved entirely to paper and WhatsApp. Trust in digital tools was at zero — the design had to earn it in the first 30 seconds.
→ Zero-onboarding became a non-negotiable principle
Insight 04
The record lock was a patient safety event.
A forgotten check-out blocked the entire patient record — no admin override, no expiry. During an insurance deadline, a locked record meant a missed submission and a billing failure. This wasn't a UX problem. It was a clinical risk.
→ Destructive lock removed, replaced with optimistic concurrency
Insight 05
No competitor had solved this. The gap was intentional.
Every competitor had encountered the home care field worker context and moved away from it. Offline-first, AI medication parsing, failed visit handling — all absent across all six tools. The space was uncontested because the problem was genuinely hard.
→ AI sig parsing prioritised in v1 as primary differentiator
Insight 06
Users said "make the app work." Research said redesign the workflow.
Every reviewer asked for a more reliable app. What they actually needed was a redesigned operational layer — digital assignment, failed visit handling, real-time admin visibility. Connectivity wasn't improvable. The design had to be connectivity-agnostic. Fixing the app without fixing the workflow would have failed again.
→ Design brief reframed: build the operational layer first

V1 scope was deliberately constrained to the core clinical loop: patient assignment → home visit → medication reconciliation → data sync. Everything outside that loop (EHR integration, prescriber messaging, AI flags) adds technical and regulatory complexity without improving the fidelity of the workflow that carries patient safety risk.

This is not feature minimalism for its own sake. It is a clinical priority decision: a tool that does one thing reliably is safer than a tool that does five things approximately.

The scope boundary also created a measurable design test: can we reduce medication reconciliation time from the legacy baseline of 4 minutes 32 seconds to under 90 seconds, with zero data loss? If the answer is yes within this scope, the case for v2 features builds itself.

01.4 — Technical Constraints & Design Givens

The Boundaries That Shaped
Every Decision

Naming a constraint is not the same as designing within one. Each constraint below is a design force: what it required, and how that requirement changed what was built.

01
Offline-First Architecture
What it is
Home visit environments have unpredictable connectivity. The application must be fully functional without an internet connection and synchronise data when connectivity returns.
Technical implementation
IndexedDB for local patient record persistence; Service Workers for background sync; conflict resolution protocol for records modified while offline.
What it demanded from design
The entire UI feedback system was designed around sync state. Local save confirmation ≠ server save confirmation. Every loading, success, and error state was designed for offline first, then adapted for connected — not the reverse.
02
HIPAA Compliance Envelope
What it is
PHI cannot be stored unencrypted on device. Session inactivity must trigger auto-logout. All transmission via TLS 1.2 minimum. Every data access requires audit logging.
Technical implementation
Web Crypto API for local storage encryption; 15-minute inactivity threshold per CMS guidelines; audit trail on all API calls.
What it demanded from design
The session expiry flow is a primary user journey, not an edge case. The auto-logout modal, re-authentication flow, and draft recovery system were all first-class design problems. The 2-minute countdown warning was iterated three times before passing confidence thresholds.
03
Zero Native App Infrastructure
What it is
No iOS/Android development capacity and no App Store presence. Delivery as a Progressive Web App — installable from a browser, built without native platform frameworks.
Technical implementation
Vercel deployment, PWA manifest, service worker registration, browser-level push notifications where supported.
What it demanded from design
Touch targets designed to web standards (44×44px minimum), not native platform HIG. The tablet-first layout had to account for both a stylus and a gloved hand — a real consideration in a clinical home visit environment.
04
WCAG 2.2 AA Mandatory
What it is
CMS-adjacent healthcare tools used by organisations receiving federal funding must meet Section 508 and WCAG 2.2 AA. This is not a recommendation. It is a legal requirement.
Technical implementation
axe-core integrated into CI/CD pipeline; semantic HTML with correct ARIA roles; keyboard navigation fully supported; colour contrast ratios ≥4.5:1 for normal text, ≥3:1 for large text.
What it demanded from design
Accessibility was a design token decision, not a post-launch audit. The entire colour system was built around contrast compliance first, then brand expression second. This constraint changed what the visual system was allowed to be.
05
Low-Bandwidth Home Environments
What it is
Reviewers operate on 3G/LTE during home visits. Large asset loads, real-time cloud sync, and data-heavy API calls are not viable during an active review session.
Technical implementation
Progressive loading; asset optimisation; "sync on return" model — patient data loads pre-visit, reconciliation data syncs post-visit.
What it demanded from design
The information architecture was shaped by bandwidth, not just clinical logic. The check-out step exists because the system needs a defined moment to download the patient record before connectivity drops. Design and architecture are the same decision in different languages.
06
Multi-Device, Browser-First
What it is
Reviewers use tablets as the primary device, laptops as secondary, and phones for emergency access. Must work across iOS Safari, Android Chrome, Windows Edge, and macOS Safari.
Technical implementation
Responsive design with tablet-first breakpoints; CSS feature detection for browser-specific rendering differences; pointer and touch event handling.
What it demanded from design
Tablet-first, not mobile-first. The medication list — the most information-dense component — was designed and tested at 768px before any other breakpoint was considered. Mobile-first scales up; tablet-first scales both directions.
07
Zero Training Budget
What it is
The design brief explicitly assumed no formal onboarding, no training session, and no help documentation available to reviewers at the point of first use.
What this rules out
No onboarding tours. No help overlays. No contextual documentation links. No assumption that a reviewer will read instructions before starting their first review.
What it demanded from design
Every label is a microcopy decision. Every error state is a teaching moment. The interface must map to how clinicians already think about medication management — mental models built in nursing school — not how a software developer would model a medication record.

06.1 — Key Insights

Seven Things the Research Told Me

From six clusters, I distilled seven design-guiding insights. These became the filter for every decision in the rewrite — if a design choice didn't address at least one of these, it didn't make the cut.

01
The tool was designed for data, not for clinical thinking
Navigation mirrored the database schema — not the clinical workflow. Reviewers had to learn the system before they could use the system. Every screen required a mental translation from "what I need to do" to "where the system put that thing."
User Interview · 9/9
02
Offline wasn't a feature — it was a survival mechanism
Every reviewer had developed a personal offline strategy: printing records, photographing screens, using paper forms as fallback. The tool's offline mode was reactive — it activated when signal was lost, not before. Reviewers needed to go offline intentionally, before leaving for the field.
Field Observation · 3 sessions
03
Admins built shadow systems to compensate for what the tool couldn't do
Every admin interviewed maintained an external tracking system — Google Sheets, Excel, email threads. This wasn't laziness or poor training. It was rational behaviour in response to a tool that had no administrative capability. The shadow systems were evidence of unmet needs, not user error.
Survey · 4/4 admins
04
Every workaround involved leaving the app
Google Maps for directions. WhatsApp for clinical notes. Calendar apps for scheduling. Excel for reporting. The tool's gaps pushed users out of the system for every high-value task. The new product had to eliminate these exits — not by blocking them, but by making the in-app experience unambiguously better.
User Interview · 9/9 used 2+ external tools
05
Medication entry was the single highest-friction interaction in the system
4m 32s average entry time. 23% error rate. 2 of 6 usability test participants abandoned the form mid-entry. The 14-field manual form wasn't just slow — it was a patient safety risk. Errors at the medication entry stage are directly linked to adverse drug events in downstream care.
Usability Testing · Round 1
06
The check-out model solved the wrong problem
Record locking was designed to prevent data conflicts. But it created a worse problem: lockouts. The actual need was data integrity — not exclusion. Optimistic locking with conflict resolution preserves integrity without the lockout risk. Five of nine reviewers had experienced mid-visit lockouts.
User Interview · 5/9 lockout incidents
07
Accessibility wasn't an edge case — it was a daily reality
Reviewers work in motion: outdoors, in poor lighting, on tablets, with gloves, with one hand occupied. The 14px touch targets weren't just a WCAG failure — they were a field failure. Designing for accessibility in this context meant designing for the hardest conditions, not the best ones.

09 — Design Principles

Six Rules I Never Broke

From the seven research insights, I distilled six design principles. Every wireframe, every component decision, every IA choice was filtered through these. If a design couldn't defend itself against at least one of these principles, it was cut.

01
Clinical-first, not data-first
Navigation and information hierarchy must mirror clinical thinking — not the database schema behind it. A reviewer opens a case to help a patient, not to browse a record system.
← Insight 01 · 9/9 interviews
02
Offline-ready by default, not by exception
Every clinical interaction must work without a network connection. Offline capability is not a feature — it is the baseline. Reviewers work in basements, rural homes, and dead zones. The app must work there too.
← Insight 02 · Field observation
03
Every workaround is a design failure
If a user leaves the app to accomplish a task the app should handle, that is not a user problem — it is a product problem. Google Maps for directions, WhatsApp for notes, Excel for reports: each is a design debt I owed the user.
← Insight 04 · 9/9 used 2+ external tools
04
Accuracy over speed in medication entry
A medication entered incorrectly is worse than a medication entered slowly. The AI sig parser optimises for accuracy first — the speed benefit is a consequence of that, not the goal. Every extracted field is presented for review before saving.
← Insight 05 · 23% error rate
05
Flexibility within structure
Clinical reality is non-linear. Patients get tired. Visits get interrupted. Assessments get deferred. The system must accommodate the messiness of real care without creating compliance gaps — every deviation needs documentation, not prevention.
← Insight 06 · 7/9 informal deferrals

10.1 — Key Design Decisions

Seven Decisions That Defined the Rewrite

These weren't aesthetic choices. Each one was a structural decision with a direct research rationale — and each one had a measurable consequence for the user.

01
Check-out model → Case ownership + optimistic locking
User Interview 5/9 lockout incidents · Avg 47 min lost per lockout
Old approach
Manual Check Out locked the entire record — preventing any other user from opening it. If the person who checked it out went offline or called in sick, the record stayed locked indefinitely. No timeout. No admin override. No recovery path.
New approach
Case assignment determines ownership. Optimistic locking detects conflicts at save time — not at open time. A Conflict Resolution UI shows both versions when conflicts occur. Admin can always reassign. No lockouts. No lost access. No data loss.
Research rationale: Hard locking solved a theoretical problem (concurrent edits) while creating a real one (lockouts). The data showed lockouts happened 5× more often than concurrent edit conflicts. We inverted the trade-off.
02
14-field manual form → AI sig parser with field-level confidence
Usability Testing 4m 32s → 47s · Error rate 23% → near zero
Old approach
All 14 medication fields entered manually from verbal patient reports, handwritten prescriptions, or printed notes. No validation, no autocomplete, no guidance. Errors were common and invisible until clinical review.
New approach
AI extracts structured fields from raw text in under 3 seconds. Each extracted field shows confidence level. Low-confidence fields highlighted in orange — reviewer gives extra attention where needed. Saves only after review. The 14-field form remains as a manual fallback.
Research rationale: StatPearls (NCBI) — 50% of medication errors occur at the prescribing/entry stage. AI-assisted entry removes the human transcription layer at the highest-risk point in the medication workflow.
03
13-section linear form → 10 modular independent assessment runners
Usability Testing 3/6 abandoned mid-assessment · 0/6 completed all 13 in one session
Old approach
Single chained 13-section form. Linear progression only. No section skipping, no pausing, no deferral mechanism. If interrupted — at section 8, say — the entire session had to restart. Progress bar showed 10% after 20 minutes of work.
New approach
Assessment Hub presents 10 individual cards — each independently startable, resumable, and deferrable. Auto-save on every answer. Estimated time per assessment shown before starting. Defer modal documents the reason when clinical reality interrupts the plan.
Research rationale: Clinical visits are non-linear. Patients get tired, have visitors, need breaks. A system that punishes interruption with data loss is not a clinical tool — it's a liability.
04
Orphaned reconciliation → integrated side-by-side with AI discrepancy detection
Field Observation 100% of observed reviewers compared lists outside the tool · Paper used in all 3 sessions
Old approach
Reconciliation existed in a separate navigation branch — completely disconnected from the medication list. No automatic comparison. Users navigated away from medications, opened reconciliation, and manually cross-referenced two lists with no tool support.
New approach
Side-by-side layout — prior medications (from referral) next to current medications (from this visit). Five discrepancy types auto-detected: missing, new, dosage mismatch, frequency mismatch, route mismatch. AI Scan provides suggested resolution with clinical rationale per discrepancy.
Research rationale: NCBI PMC systematic review — 30% of older home healthcare patients have potential medication errors. Auto-detection at the reconciliation stage is the most direct intervention available at the point of care.
05
Binary offline toggle → proactive pre-trip sync with 5-category progress
Field Observation Reviewer arrived at patient home with blank screens in 2 of 3 observed sessions
Old approach
A toggle at login: Online or Offline. No confirmation of what was cached. No granularity. If it failed silently — which it did regularly — the reviewer discovered it at the patient's door, not at the office.
New approach
Pre-Trip Sync screen gives explicit control: choose this case, all active cases, or all scheduled cases. A 7-step animated log shows exactly what's being encrypted and transferred. A persistent Sync Status Pill shows live sync state at all times. Auto-sync on reconnect. Per-item retry on failure.
Research rationale: The existing model was reactive — it responded to lost connectivity. Field observation showed reviewers needed to declare offline intent before leaving the office. Transparency in sync builds clinical trust.
06
No admin interface → full RBAC with role-specific dashboards
User Interview 4/4 admins maintained shadow systems · Avg morning review: 38 minutes
Old approach
Single user role. Admins accessed the same interface as reviewers with no additional capability. Case creation required IT. Assignment required phone calls. Reports required Excel. Operations ran entirely outside the tool.
New approach
Two distinct role experiences at every level — routing, dashboard, navigation, case actions, data access. Admin dashboard built from scratch with KPI strip, Needs Attention panel, Unassigned Queue, Reviewer Performance reports, and full case lifecycle management.
Research rationale: Shadow systems are not user error — they are evidence of design debt. The 47-column spreadsheet existed because the tool created a vacuum. We filled the vacuum with purpose-built admin tooling.
07
WCAG as afterthought → WCAG 2.2 AA as design foundation
Secondary Research Legacy system: 14px touch targets · Zero focus rings · Missing form labels throughout
Old approach
Accessibility was not considered in any phase of the legacy build. Color contrast failed at multiple critical touchpoints. Form inputs had no associated labels. Focus states were invisible. On tablet, touch targets measured as small as 14px.
New approach
WCAG 2.2 AA compliance built into every component from day one. Minimum 44×44px touch targets. Visible focus rings on all interactive elements. Color never sole indicator of meaning. axe-core integrated into CI/CD — zero critical violations gate deployment.
Research rationale: ADA Title III + Section 508 compliance is a legal requirement for healthcare software. Beyond compliance — reviewers work in motion, outdoors, on tablets. Accessibility standards are the minimum safe operating conditions for real field use.

14 — Reflection

What I Learned From Rebuilding Everything

A rewrite is the most expensive design intervention possible. It only makes sense when the foundation is wrong — not just the surface. Here's what I'd do the same, what I'd do differently, and where this product goes next.

What worked — keep doing this
Field observation before wireframes. Watching a reviewer use the legacy app on a patient's kitchen table told me more in 4 hours than 2 weeks of stakeholder interviews could have. The paper-list comparison moment changed the entire reconciliation design.
Building the design system before building pages. The MEDCARE token system meant every page was consistent from day one. No colour discrepancies, no spacing drift, no component inconsistency between screens.
Two rounds of usability testing. Round 1 on the legacy system gave me a documented baseline with real numbers. Round 2 on the prototype gave me validation before handoff. Both were essential — one without the other would have been incomplete.
Making the rewrite case with data, not opinion. Every stakeholder conversation was grounded in specific research findings. "23% error rate" and "4 of 4 admins maintain shadow spreadsheets" are much harder to dismiss than "users find it frustrating."
↺ What I'd do differently
Card sorting earlier for IA validation. I ran the IA past users through interviews — but a structured card sort with 8–10 participants would have given me quantitative confidence in the navigation model before prototyping began. I'd add this in week 1.
Prototype the offline sync UX before build, not alongside it. The Pre-Trip Sync screen was designed at the same time the service worker was being built. Testing a low-fidelity sync prototype first would have surfaced the "7-step log" insight earlier and saved iteration cycles.
Earlier accessibility audit — not just a final pass. While WCAG was baked into every component, a formal audit with an assistive technology user in week 2 (not week 4) would have caught the keyboard navigation gaps in the assessment runner earlier in the process.
A dedicated admin research session in week 1. Admin needs were surfaced through interviews — but all four admin participants were interviewed in week 2, after reviewer research was done. Running admin and reviewer research in parallel from day 1 would have shaped the IA decision sooner.
